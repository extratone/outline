Years of complaints against Cambridge Analytica reveal how it influenced voters

Complaints filed by U.S. citizens from 2013-2017 detail Cambridge Analytica’s messaging tactics.

Paris Martineau Mar—28—2018 03:58PM EST

The Outline has uncovered a number of previously unpublished harassment complaints filed against Cambridge Analytica and SCL in response to the numerous political survey and messaging calls made on behalf of U.S. campaigns between 2013 and 2017. A significant portion of the complaints filed reference calls received in early-to-mid 2014, the same time period during which Cambridge Analytica provided “campaign strategy and messaging advice” to Republican campaigns in Colorado, Oregon, North Carolina, New Hampshire, and Arkansas, according to documents obtained by the Washington Post.

The complaints describe the companies’ data-gathering methods in great detail, which reportedly ranged from loaded political (and personal) surveys, paid focus groups, and sentiment analysis questionnaires. While there were a considerable number of complaints filed with the FCC over SCL’s predatory conduct, most U.S. citizens voiced their concerns online on popular self-reporting sites, such as 800Notes.

Most took issue with the frequency and timing of Cambridge Analytica’s calls — which came at all hours, multiple times a day — and the invasive, often personal, questions the company posed. The firm’s constant harassment and shady tactics left most users suspicious of Cambridge Analytica’s intentions. “What is their political agenda? Who is their client?” one Oregon resident asked after receiving multiple calls inviting them to join a focus group on “race, nsa, abortion, gays.”

Though the use of a third party political consulting firm to conduct polls and surveys is in no way revolutionary, Cambridge Analytica isn’t just any consulting firm. Infamous for siphoning millions of users’ Facebook data and (allegedly) influencing voters around the world, Cambridge Analytica (and its parent company SCL) has been described as a kind of “psyops contractor.” Whistleblower Christopher Wylie alleges that the company exists merely to allow politics-obsessed billionaires, like Robert Mercer or Vincent Tchenguiz, to skirt FEC regulations by passing their money to contractors like SCL, rather than giving directly to campaigns.

The majority of the calls referenced Republican candidates who had enlisted Cambridge Analytica and SCL’s services in 2014. One Colorado resident described the numerous calls they had received from “SCL Elections,” which often took the form of a four question “political survey.” The caller noted that the surveys often mentioned the then-candidate Cory Gardner, who went on to defeat incumbent Democratic Senator Mark Udall in the 2014 Colorado Senate election later that year:

Political survey. I have gotten several of these calls. They all claim to be four question surveys. The first question was am I a Republican or Democrat. Second was do I vote every election. Third questions was do I always vote party line. Fourth question was how excited am I about the upcoming election. Subsequent calls have been variations on this theme but they usually ask me if I would vote for Cory Gardiner for Governor. I assume these are funded by his committee and if you answer yes you'll be asked for a campaign contribution. Other variations have asked me how conservative I am and how conservative I think Cory Gardiner is. So it may also be a Tancredo poll since he's claiming to be the true conservative candidate.

Cambridge Analytica (which worked alongside SCL Elections) took credit for Gardner’s success in a 2014 post-election report, first published by the Washington Post. The report says that Cambridge Analytica and SCL played a central role in Republican elections in Colorado, Oregon, North Carolina, New Hampshire, Arkansas, and others.

The Cambridge Analytica document lists phone calls as the primary method of voter conversion and mobilization. “Given the limited financial resources available to the team, the GOTV effort consisted mostly of paid calling and attempting to mobilize some volunteers to make persuasion calls,” says the section on Oregon.

According to the report, the phone surveys Cambridge Analytica conducted in Arkansas, North Carolina and New Hampshire for Ambassador John Bolton’s super PAC were highly effective.

“CA-SCL contracted an external firm to conduct a phone survey to test attitudes amongst target populations, which also provided significant evidence of the efficacy of CA-SCL’s messaging,” reads the post-election report. “Relative to an uncontacted control group, respondents in the study who had been targeted with Bolton Super PAC messaging on foreign policy and national security showed a significant increase in their awareness of these subjects.”
